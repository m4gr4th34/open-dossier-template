the turn

Now turn JavaScript off. It's still here.

That is the part a toy can't do. The figure you just dragged is also a sealed, static SVG baked into the page — it renders with no JavaScript at all, it is byte-stable, and it is exactly what gets archived and timestamped. The interaction is an enhancement layered on top of a permanent picture, not a replacement for one. Three honest layers:

  • floor a sealed, static, archival SVG poster — no JavaScript, hash-stable, the thing that gets sealed and timestamped. It is what a reader with scripts off, a screen reader, or an archive crawler sees.
  • source the figure's generator, carried in plain sight as a data-figure JSON spec — the few hundred bytes a person or an AI can read, diff, and edit to change the figure.
  • ceiling the live interaction — drag, zoom, play — progressive enhancement that the runtime upgrades the floor into when scripts are available.

So a living figure is alive and permanent: it moves when a reader is present to move it, and it freezes into a citable, archival artifact when no one is. Nothing here is a widget that breaks in an archive five years from now.

the depth

And it generalizes.

The cosmic zoom isn't a one-off. It is five ordinary living figures, composed. Each stands on its own:

Deterministic physics. Kepler's equation is solved per body, per frame — no pre-baked path. The eight planets are about 1.4 KB of orbital elements; the asteroid and Kuiper belts are a 203-byte pair of seeds that scatter two thousand bodies at view time, and the Oort cloud is another 68 bytes for nine hundred more.

A galaxy from a seed. A four-arm spiral of nine thousand stars, plus a bulge and a halo, unfolded from a single 760-byte spec — not a stored point cloud, a generator. Change the seed and you get a different, equally lawful galaxy.

One step out: a real catalog. The two bright spirals are the Milky Way ("you are here") and Andromeda, the dominant pair ~2.5 Mly apart — a real named-galaxy catalog, with a seeded dwarf swarm filling in around them.

Foam, not a grid. Galaxies and clusters strung along filaments between dark voids — a node-and-filament seeded foam over a real cluster catalog. The faint strands are the filaments; the dim points are the galaxies that gather along them, leaving the voids dark.

Where sight stops. Past the cosmic web the clustering washes out into a uniform, isotropic field, bounded by the observable horizon. The zoom ends where sight ends — not where the universe ends.

The thread through all six: figures store generators, not output. The runtime is vendored, pinned, and zero-dependency, and the render modules compose a small set of shared primitives — the Kepler solve, the seeded scatter, the logarithmic zoom — rather than reimplementing them. The cosmic zoom reuses all five — the orrery, the galaxy, the Local Group, the cosmic web, and the observable universe — the very same way. Small inputs, lawful math, one runtime: that is what keeps a figure both light enough to archive and faithful enough to trust.

the honesty note

A capability that invites — not a bar that excludes.

None of this means a result has to be beautiful to be worthy. A null result, a flat line, a boring-but-correct table gets the same sealed, first-class frame as a galaxy you can drag. The living figure is an offer, not a requirement: when the truth happens to be a slider you pull, pull it; when the truth is a number you simply have to sit with, the format sits with it just as honestly.

The point of the medium is to make truth magnetic, never to dress up a finding it hasn't earned. Spectacle never outranks honesty here — every label stays true, whether the figure soars or sits still. A showcase that forgot this would be selling animation; this one is showing a way to tell the truth and keep it.

the invitation

Fork it and publish.

There's no plug-in to install and no platform to join. The whole capability is a vendored runtime and a handful of plain-text specs sitting in a public repository — and this page is just six sealed demos in <iframe>s. If you have a result worth reading, you can have figures like these under it by the end of the afternoon.

The work makes the argument. Start from the template: github.com/m4gr4th34/open-dossier-template — the README's Rituals walk you from "Use this template" to a live, sealed, timestamped dossier. Then publish, and let the figures do the talking.
