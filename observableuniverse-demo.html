The Observable Universe — where the zoom honestly stops

The ending. Past the cosmic web there is no larger structure: the universe goes homogeneous and isotropic — the same in every direction, with no centre. The zoom stops at the observable horizon, the oldest light we can see. Rendered by figures/observableuniverse.js on the same primitives in figures/figures.js.

Drag to pan, scroll to zoom, or jump to a region. At the largest scales the clustering of the cosmic web washes out: galaxies fill the field evenly, the same in every direction — the cosmological principle. There is nothing bigger to zoom into. The warm ring is the observable horizon — the last-scattering surface (the cosmic microwave background), the furthest light has had time to reach us, ~46.5 billion light-years away. The zoom stops there. It is the limit of what we can see, not the edge of what exists: the universe extends beyond — we simply cannot observe it. And "you are here" sits at the centre only because every observer's horizon is centred on them; there is no centre to the universe, and the view is the same from everywhere.

A standalone demo, not one of the frozen editions — and the honest ending of the zoom. The field is uniform and isotropic: no clustering, no filaments, no preferred direction, and no invented larger structure — the cosmological principle, not fabricated nesting. The horizon ring is labelled the observable horizon, never the edge of the universe: it is the limit of what we can see, and the universe extends beyond it. Live-only by design — no sealed poster of its own; the composed cosmic figure's JS-off floor stays the Milky Way.
